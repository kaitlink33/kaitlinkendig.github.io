// About Me

Who I Am & What Drives Me

"The best security work is invisible. Not because it hides, rather because it's done right. My mission is to build systems and practices resilient enough to disappear into the background."

Background

Where I Come From

My path into technology started with a fundamental question: why do systems fail? That curiosity led me deep into the world of Linux environments, network infrastructure, and eventually security operations. I didn't just want to use systems. I also wanted to understand them at every layer.

I've spent time working in technical support, operations, and incident response roles. Each sharpened a different edge of my skill set. From managing tickets & escalating issues, to tracing the root cause of a security alert, I've learned to provide solutions when things get complicated.

How I Work

I'm a command-line native. I believe the terminal is the most honest interface. It is rather efficient, and does tasks directly. I write Python for automation and analysis, Java for structured problem-solving, and I've built a genuine appreciation for the discipline that Linux requires.

When I approach a problem, I start by mapping it. Whether that's a network topology, an incident timeline, a devlopment project, or a gap analysis against a security framework, I want the full picture before I start making changes. That habit has saved me from many well-intentioned mistakes.

"Systems don't fail randomly — they fail predictably, at the seams we didn't design carefully enough."

What I'm Working Toward

I'm particularly drawn to cloud infrastructure and its security implications. The shift to distributed, dynamic environments has fundamentally changed what it means to defend a network — and I find that challenge genuinely exciting. I'm building skills in cloud architecture, gap analysis methodologies, and network topology design with a long view toward security engineering roles.

Beyond the technical, I care about communication. Security teams fail not because they lack tools, but because they lack shared understanding. I work to translate technical findings into clear, actionable language for anyone who needs to make decisions.

Outside the Commandline

When I'm not at a keyboard, I'm usually thinking about systems of a different kind — logistics, data trends, how large organizations coordinate. I find that reading broadly about how complex systems are functionally managed (and mismanaged) makes me a better engineer. The patterns are often the same.
